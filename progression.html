Phase 01: Initial Direction

The Branding Experiment

The investigation began with a broad, largely unresolved line of enquiry: how branding operates on the human body, and what ethical implications emerge when identity itself becomes something that can be constructed, labelled, and consumed. At this early stage, the approach was heavily influenced by references such as Marina Abramović’s Rhythm 0, alongside a general interest in consumer culture. However, the initial intelligence gathering remained surface-level, with concepts forming more from instinct than structured analysis.

The proposed direction centred around a participatory installation. The concept imagined a mannequin as a physical stand-in, where audiences could apply brands or markers onto the body. The intention was to expose how identity is shaped externally, but the execution lacked clarity. The idea existed as a provocation rather than a fully realised system, with little consideration for how meaning would be measured or interpreted beyond the immediate interaction.

Looking back, this phase reveals a critical starting point: the project was driven by an interest in what branding does, but not yet how it operates in practice. There was a clear thematic motive, but no defined methodology, no structured mechanics, and no understanding of how an audience would engage beyond surface participation. This uncertainty was significant but necessary. It exposed the gap between concept and execution, setting the foundation for a shift from a passive observational installation to an active, system-driven investigation.

Phase 02: Exhibition Encounter

The Delusion — 05/11/25

A field reconnaissance visit to Danielle Brathwaite-Shirley’s The Delusion at the Serpentine Gallery introduced a radical alternative to passive participation. The exhibition operated as a “human engine,” demanding active engagement, negotiation, and forced decision-making between participants. While not immediately integrated into the active investigation, this encounter provided critical operational intelligence that would later trigger a total redirection of the project.

The experience was immersive and inherently unsettling. The environment weaponised an eerie, almost horror-like aesthetic, deploying multiplayer game systems that forced players to act under live conditions. One specific mechanic, The Border game, proved vital. Players acted as gatekeepers, deciding who was granted entry to a space based on severely limited, fragmented intelligence. Judgements had to be rapid. Furthermore, these decisions were public; the awareness of being observed by other 'agents' introduced a layer of social pressure, forcing choices to feel immediate, exposed, and consequential.

This dynamic exposed a critical flaw in the initial project direction. While the original mannequin concept relied on audience interaction, it lacked commitment and accountability. The Delusion demonstrated how interactive frameworks can trap players in positions where their inherent biases are revealed through forced action. The velocity and pressure of the decision-making mirrored real-world hostile environments, specifically digital spaces where judgements are frequently rapid and highly public.

This encounter did not instantly close the case on the installation route. Initially logged as an isolated reference point, its structural significance only emerged in retrospect. However, it successfully established a new operational standard: a model of participation that was active, uncomfortable, and socially exposed. This intelligence became the primary catalyst when the investigation finally pivoted away from passive observation, proving that the most effective way to expose bias is to place the subject inside the system itself.

Phase 03: Proposal Submission & Early Friction

Proposal and ICO - 13/11/25

The submission of the initial proposal marked a moment of apparent clarity, but in reality exposed the instability of the project’s foundation. Titled The Manufactured Self, the proposal positioned the work as a participatory installation exploring the ethical implications of branding the human body. It drew on a wide range of theoretical frameworks, including Naomi Klein’s No Logo, Erving Goffman’s Gender Advertisements, and Jean Baudrillard’s The Consumer Society, alongside artistic references such as Marina Abramović’s Rhythm 0. On paper, the project appeared conceptually rich and academically grounded.

However, this clarity was largely superficial. While the written proposal articulated a strong research question and theoretical positioning, the practical execution remained underdeveloped. The central idea, inviting participants to “brand” mannequins, had not been fully resolved in terms of interaction design, audience behaviour, or ethical implications. The participatory element, which was positioned as the core of the project, lacked clear definition and structure.

Tutor feedback reinforced this tension. While the proposal was recognised as engaging and well-referenced, key concerns emerged around the ambiguity of participation, specifically whether the project functioned as co-creation within a marketing framework or as an experimental participatory methodology. Ethical considerations were also identified as insufficiently developed, particularly in relation to audience involvement and consent. Additionally, the project timeline was described as overly broad, lacking detailed milestones and failing to account for processes such as ethical clearance and iterative testing.

Despite receiving an overall approval with modifications, the feedback revealed a critical issue: the project was conceptually ambitious but structurally unclear. At this stage, there was a growing awareness that the idea had been developed more as a theoretical proposition than a fully considered experience. The proposal functioned as a starting point rather than a resolved direction, highlighting the need for deeper research, clearer methodology, and a more defined approach to participation.

In retrospect, this phase represents a moment of false coherence, where strong academic framing masked a lack of practical resolution. This tension would become increasingly significant as the project progressed, ultimately contributing to a shift away from passive installation towards more active, system-based forms of engagement.

Phase 05: Methodological Reorientation

Winter Pause — Dec '25 - Jan '26

The winter operational pause functioned as a period of strategic reassessment rather than active field production. Following the structural vulnerabilities exposed in Supervision Log 01, this phase was dedicated to methodological recalibration, primarily guided by Patricia Leavy’s Method Meets Art.

This intelligence facilitated a critical reframing: abandoning the ambiguous participatory installation in favour of a defined, arts-based research protocol, where the creative system itself serves as the primary investigative mechanism.

While no physical prototypes or live testing were deployed during this window, the conceptual framework underwent a fundamental shift. Preliminary theories began to surface regarding structured, forced-decision frameworks, specifically exploring the mechanics of game systems. Though still in early theoretical stages, this marked the definitive pivot toward an active, system-led methodology, setting the parameters for the next phase of physical testing.

Phase 06: Live Prototyping & Critique

Word Association Workshop — Jan '26

Moving from theoretical planning to active testing, this phase executed the first primary research experiment during a live group critique. Guided by preceding supervision, the methodology shifted towards live audience participation to expose unconscious bias in real-time.

The first iteration of the project took the form of a rapid word association workshop centred on a deliberately ambiguous AI-generated human figure. The image was minimal in setting and expression to reduce overt narrative cues. Participants, including the investigator, were asked to record immediate descriptive and interpretive words without self-censorship.

Initial responses appeared observational: clothing, posture, background. However, even in this constrained format, assumptions quickly emerged. Physical traits were inferred despite the knowledge that the image was artificially generated. After a moment of hesitation triggered by the word ‘blank’, more emotionally loaded terms by the investigator surfaced, including evaluative judgments about expression and personality. This demonstrated that neutrality in representation is difficult to sustain; the absence of narrative detail often intensifies projection rather than suppressing it.

When the exercise expanded to group participation, assumptions escalated. Words referencing class, lifestyle, morality, and ideology appeared rapidly and confidently. Bias became socially reinforced: once one interpretation was voiced, others followed. This revealed that unconscious bias is not only an individual cognitive phenomenon but a collectively amplified process.

However, this format had limitations. The exercise was overtly analytical and risked positioning participants as subjects within a bias ‘test’. Awareness of observation potentially altered behaviour. While the workshop successfully surfaced projection, it lacked narrative immersion and procedural structure.

Phase 07: System Re-Engineering

The Board Game Pivot — Feb '26

Following the operational limitations exposed during Critique 01, the investigation entered a secondary reflective phase. The objective was to identify a framework that successfully removed the overt "test" conditions of the previous workshop. This phase marks the critical conceptual breakthrough: translating the investigation into a physical board game format.

[ INVESTIGATOR LOG: 014 ]

It should be noted for the official record that this major structural breakthrough did not occur during structured academic analysis, but rather during an off-duty conceptual anomaly. Specifically: a shower.

The structural limitations of the word association workshop, its lack of narrative immersion and replayability, necessitated a format change. The board game format was identified as the ideal delivery mechanism. Instead of participants remaining hyper-aware of being observed, unconscious bias would be securely embedded within a system of rules, strategic decisions, and social interactions. This reframed the participatory act entirely: from something observational and self-conscious to an experiential procedure where bias emerges as a natural byproduct of gameplay.

Early architectural thinking centred around players making rapid judgments on ambiguous character profiles, navigating identity and perception through forced decision-making. This operational direction was corroborated through field testing of existing commercial models. Playing Deception: Murder in Hong Kong with associates demonstrated precisely how social deduction mechanics can shape behaviour, heavily influence interpretation, and manufacture collective meaning.

While the concept remained theoretically confined at this stage, it represented the final and decisive shift away from 'branding the body' installations. Unconscious bias would no longer be passively observed; it would be explored as a playable, interactive system.

Phase 08: Live Simulation

Critique 02: The Staged Scenario — 09/02/26

The investigation then transitioned from theoretical mapping to a live, system-led operation. Deploying a structured crime scenario complete with suspect profiles and material evidence, this phase tested how subjects construct narrative when confined by specific rules of engagement.

The operation staged a fictional homicide, presenting participants with two primary suspects, curated branded artifacts, and contextual intelligence. Unlike earlier unstructured tests, this simulation forced interaction through active decision-making. Crucially, the factual cause of death, anaphylactic shock triggered by a peanut-based protein bar, was explicitly seeded within the provided evidence. The parameters were designed to test interpretation, not discovery.

Despite the presence of objective forensic data, surveillance of the test subjects revealed a consistent anomaly: a collective rejection of the facts. Participants overwhelmingly pursued a narrative of intentional poisoning. The desire for a coherent, malicious motive entirely overrode the established evidence. Furthermore, subjects weaponised mundane lifestyle markers, gym culture, protein consumption, and institutional branding, elevating them into concrete indicators of guilt without any logical foundation.

This yielded a critical intelligence breakthrough. Unconscious bias is not merely a reflexive visual association; it is systematically manufactured by narrative frameworks and rule-based constraints. By formalising the investigation into a 'game,' the system effectively laundered the participants' prejudices, transforming baseless suspicion into a sanctioned operational mechanic. The board game format was officially validated as the optimal mechanism for exposing the collective construction of meaning.

Phase 09: Operational Refinement

System Consolidation — March '26

During the month, the investigation shifted from active live-testing to systemic consolidation. Field reconnaissance at the Young V&A provided critical intelligence on how analogue game structures enforce behavioural compliance. The focus narrowed onto role assignment, hidden information, and social deduction as mechanisms to actively engineer decision-making.

Deepen analysis of existing game systems to understand how they force transitions between intuitive intuition and deliberate deduction. Bias must function as the core operating system, not a mere byproduct.

By the execution of Critique 03 on 23/03/26, the project’s priority had shifted entirely toward stress-testing these underlying mechanics rather than presenting a resolved aesthetic artifact. The period was utilised to scrutinise the relational structure between suspect cards, evidence constraints, and the subsequent participant judgments.

While this iteration appeared less visually resolved than prior tests, it represented a vital structural turning point. The project ceased being a collection of isolated conceptual experiments and transitioned into the foundational architecture of a fully functional, manipulative interactive framework. The groundwork for the final prototype had been laid.

Phase 10: System Resolution

Easter Operational Window — April '26

The Easter break marked a period of sustained structural development. The simulation's architecture was resolved into a cohesive multi-round framework, guiding participants through introduction, investigation, interrogation, and a final verdict. Crucially, the narrative world and branded elements were synchronised to operate within a highly believable, closed system.

Simultaneously, the visual hierarchy was strictly regulated. Decisions regarding typography, colour grading, and layout were intentionally restrained, favouring clinical neutrality. The objective was to avoid over-directing the subjects, ensuring that prejudice emerged organically through interaction rather than being explicitly prescribed by the game's design.

Phase 11: Digital & Physical Execution

Infrastructure & Supervision — May '26

By May 2026, the investigation entered an intensive production phase. Operations were split between constructing a bespoke digital archive (this current interface) and translating the conceptual game mechanics into tangible, exhibition-ready evidence. A critical briefing on 05/05/26 established the final parameters for live field testing and material fabrication.

Significant operational time was dedicated to engineering the digital portfolio. To ensure the platform functioned as an authentic extension of the Face Value methodology, standard commercial website templates were rejected. Instead, a fully custom architecture was developed using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. This required iterative testing of responsive layouts, modal dossier systems, animated transitions, and expanding classified logs. The development process became highly performative; the code itself acted as a creative medium, mirroring the project’s core themes of surveillance, restricted access, and procedural investigation.
