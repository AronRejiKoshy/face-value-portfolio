Phase 01: Initial Direction

The Branding Experiment

The investigation began with a broad, largely unresolved line of enquiry: how branding operates on the human body, and what ethical implications emerge when identity itself becomes something that can be constructed, labelled, and consumed. At this early stage, the approach was heavily influenced by references such as Marina Abramović’s Rhythm 0, alongside a general interest in consumer culture. However, the initial intelligence gathering remained surface-level, with concepts forming more from instinct than structured analysis.

The proposed direction centred around a participatory installation. The concept imagined a mannequin as a physical stand-in, where audiences could apply brands or markers onto the body. The intention was to expose how identity is shaped externally, but the execution lacked clarity. The idea existed as a provocation rather than a fully realised system, with little consideration for how meaning would be measured or interpreted beyond the immediate interaction.

Looking back, this phase reveals a critical starting point: the project was driven by an interest in what branding does, but not yet how it operates in practice. There was a clear thematic motive, but no defined methodology, no structured mechanics, and no understanding of how an audience would engage beyond surface participation. This uncertainty was significant but necessary. It exposed the gap between concept and execution, setting the foundation for a shift from a passive observational installation to an active, system-driven investigation.

Phase 02: Exhibition Encounter

The Delusion — 05/11/25

A field reconnaissance visit to Danielle Brathwaite-Shirley’s The Delusion at the Serpentine Gallery introduced a radical alternative to passive participation. The exhibition operated as a “human engine,” demanding active engagement, negotiation, and forced decision-making between participants. While not immediately integrated into the active investigation, this encounter provided critical operational intelligence that would later trigger a total redirection of the project.

The experience was immersive and inherently unsettling. The environment weaponised an eerie, almost horror-like aesthetic, deploying multiplayer game systems that forced players to act under live conditions. One specific mechanic, The Border game, proved vital. Players acted as gatekeepers, deciding who was granted entry to a space based on severely limited, fragmented intelligence. Judgements had to be rapid. Furthermore, these decisions were public; the awareness of being observed by other 'agents' introduced a layer of social pressure, forcing choices to feel immediate, exposed, and consequential.

This dynamic exposed a critical flaw in the initial project direction. While the original mannequin concept relied on audience interaction, it lacked commitment and accountability. The Delusion demonstrated how interactive frameworks can trap players in positions where their inherent biases are revealed through forced action. The velocity and pressure of the decision-making mirrored real-world hostile environments, specifically digital spaces where judgements are frequently rapid and highly public.

This encounter did not instantly close the case on the installation route. Initially logged as an isolated reference point, its structural significance only emerged in retrospect. However, it successfully established a new operational standard: a model of participation that was active, uncomfortable, and socially exposed. This intelligence became the primary catalyst when the investigation finally pivoted away from passive observation, proving that the most effective way to expose bias is to place the subject inside the system itself.

Phase 03: Proposal Submission & Early Friction

Proposal and ICO - 13/11/25

The submission of the initial proposal marked a moment of apparent clarity, but in reality exposed the instability of the project’s foundation. Titled The Manufactured Self, the proposal positioned the work as a participatory installation exploring the ethical implications of branding the human body. It drew on a wide range of theoretical frameworks, including Naomi Klein’s No Logo, Erving Goffman’s Gender Advertisements, and Jean Baudrillard’s The Consumer Society, alongside artistic references such as Marina Abramović’s Rhythm 0. On paper, the project appeared conceptually rich and academically grounded.

However, this clarity was largely superficial. While the written proposal articulated a strong research question and theoretical positioning, the practical execution remained underdeveloped. The central idea, inviting participants to “brand” mannequins, had not been fully resolved in terms of interaction design, audience behaviour, or ethical implications. The participatory element, which was positioned as the core of the project, lacked clear definition and structure.

Tutor feedback reinforced this tension. While the proposal was recognised as engaging and well-referenced, key concerns emerged around the ambiguity of participation, specifically whether the project functioned as co-creation within a marketing framework or as an experimental participatory methodology. Ethical considerations were also identified as insufficiently developed, particularly in relation to audience involvement and consent. Additionally, the project timeline was described as overly broad, lacking detailed milestones and failing to account for processes such as ethical clearance and iterative testing.

Despite receiving an overall approval with modifications, the feedback revealed a critical issue: the project was conceptually ambitious but structurally unclear. At this stage, there was a growing awareness that the idea had been developed more as a theoretical proposition than a fully considered experience. The proposal functioned as a starting point rather than a resolved direction, highlighting the need for deeper research, clearer methodology, and a more defined approach to participation.

In retrospect, this phase represents a moment of false coherence, where strong academic framing masked a lack of practical resolution. This tension would become increasingly significant as the project progressed, ultimately contributing to a shift away from passive installation towards more active, system-based forms of engagement.
