ARCHIVE NO. 441A

System Overview & Mechanics

Concept Directive

Face Value is a social deduction board game designed to explore how players construct suspicion from limited, brand-coded information. Players are presented with a crime case and a set of suspects. Through interaction, discussion, and evidence collection, they must determine who is responsible. The system is engineered to reveal how narrative and perception override factual reasoning.

Player Role Parameters

Each player assumes the identity of a suspect connected to the victim. Players are not assigned as the murderer. Instead, they participate in the process of investigation, discussion, and accusation. Their role is to interpret evidence, form narratives, and influence collective judgment.

Operational Flow

  1. Setup: Players are assigned character profiles and initial context is given.
  2. Investigation: Players move between locations, collecting evidence and are allowed to use character abilities.
  3. Discussion: Players share interpretations and form suspicions based on available information.
  4. Judgment: Players return to a central location and vote on who they believe is responsible.
  5. Reveal: The true cause of the crime and outcome is revealed.

Core Mechanic Vulnerability

The system is structured to prioritize interpretation over objective truth. Evidence is distributed in a way that requires cross-referencing, yet players are more likely to form conclusions based on character perception and narrative coherence. This creates a space where branding, lifestyle cues, and identity markers influence judgment more strongly than factual evidence.

ARCHIVE NO. 441B

Physical Apparatus

Spatial Layout

  • Location Tiles: 5-6 tiles representing physical spaces (Example: Gym, café, apartment, university space, shopping mall).
  • Central Space: "Court" or Discussion Area (can be adapted based on the specific case parameters).

Voting System

Public Voting: Tokens placed on suspects.
Private Recording Sheet: Questions/prompts to guide the player (utilised as technical depth for data extraction).

Movement System

Each round, the player chooses where they want to go. They can use their "ability" to draw relevant evidence based on the location tile.

Evidence Cards

Object cards. Ideally, these are the branded objects affiliated with the victim, the suspect, or other players. Content cards provide more information about location or timeline hints.

Character Cards

Profiles containing baseline data: Portrait, Name, Occupation, Lifestyle markers, Traits, Flaws, and unique "Abilities". Designed to trigger instant profiling.

Protocol: Web Development

Application: Custom Digital Architecture

To effectively present this project, standard PDF formats or drag-and-drop website builders were entirely insufficient. The investigation required building a custom digital portfolio from the ground up to simulate a secure, classified terminal. Over a highly condensed 2-3 week period, I had to rapidly learn the fundamentals of web coding (HTML, CSS, and JavaScript) to translate my conceptual designs into a functional, interactive website.

[ INVESTIGATOR LOG: TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE ]

It should be noted for the official record that this rapid upskilling was heavily supplemented by 'technical associates' (my Computer Science flatmates). Their expertise was frequently subpoenaed for initial setup, interpreting error codes, and late-night debugging interventions when the system inevitably broke.

To accelerate this process, I also deployed Cursor, an AI-powered code editor. For someone without a formal computer science background, this functioned like a highly advanced investigative assistant. It helped predict formatting structures, write foundational code blocks, and translate complex error logs into plain English. While it didn't build the site for me, it dramatically reduced the friction of learning syntax, allowing me to focus on the overarching system architecture and visual design rather than getting trapped by a missing semicolon.

Operational Logging (GitHub)

Given the rapid trial-and-error nature of learning to code, maintaining a constant backup of the operation was paramount. All site updates were continuously logged and saved through GitHub. While the tracking notes (commits) reflect the slightly chaotic reality of building a site from scratch, the repository acts as an honest, chronological evidence log of the platform's evolution.

The Web Framework

Building the terminal required juggling three different coding languages simultaneously, each handling a specific part of the operation. I had to learn how they interact to stop the archive from collapsing.

HTML // THE FOUNDATION

This is the digital filing cabinet. HTML provides the structural 'bones' of the site. I had to learn how to organize the content so the browser actually understands what is a heading, what is a button, and what is classified text. Without this, the archive would just be an unreadable pile of data.

CSS // THE PAINT

Translating the visual identity into code. This is what makes the interface look like a redacted surveillance terminal rather than a boring Word document. I had to learn how to force grids, spacing, and colours to behave, frequently calling my 'associates' when the layouts inexplicably broke.

JS // THE ELECTRICITY

Adding the interactive behaviour. JavaScript is the logic that brings the terminal to life, allowing you to click between tabs, 'decrypt' logs, and open evidence pop-ups. It required the most trial and error (and the most panicked emergency calls) to get right without breaking everything else.

THE HUB // SYSTEM NAVIGATION

The culmination of the web development process. An early iteration of the central evidence board that ties the entire digital archive together, utilising precise CSS positioning to create an immersive, tactile navigation system for the user.

ARCHIVE NO. 441E

Protocol: Figma

Application: The Digital Evidence Board

While Figma is conventionally used for high-fidelity UI prototyping, my application of the software was much more structural. Rather than designing pixel-perfect website screens, I utilised Figma as an infinite digital canvas, essentially a digital 'evidence board'. It served as the primary sorting facility to organize scattered research, map out game mechanics, and categorize visual assets before translating them into functional code or physical print.

Spatial Organization

By creating a strict page-based hierarchy within the file, I was able to transform a chaotic collection of ideas into an easily navigable digital space. This bird's-eye view of the project was critical for maintaining continuity between the physical board game components and the digital archive.

ARCHIVE NO. 441F

Protocol: Adobe Illustrator

Application: Vector Identity & Logo Generation

To establish a credible institutional authority for Face Value, the project required a definitive logo mark. Having never used Adobe Illustrator extensively before, I had to rapidly learn the fundamentals of vector graphics. I dedicated myself to learning the essential tools, manipulating paths, adjusting anchor points, and using the shape builder, to construct a clean, scalable primary logo. It was a steep learning curve of trial, error, and wrestling with vectors to produce a mark that felt authentic and authoritative.

Vector Construction

Translating conceptual ideas into scalable vector shapes. This phase required learning how to control bezier curves and geometric paths to craft a symbol that felt both official and slightly unsettling, perfectly fitting the investigative theme without losing legibility at smaller scales.

Typographic Integration

Pairing the newly constructed logo mark with the project's established typography. The primary challenge was balancing the visual weight of the vector symbol alongside the bold letterforms of the title, ensuring the branding looked seamless across both the physical evidence cards and the digital terminal.

ARCHIVE NO. 441G

Protocol: Adobe Photoshop

Application: Component Fabrication & Layout

While Figma handled the overarching system architecture, Adobe Photoshop was deployed as the primary production engine for the physical board game components. This is where the simulation became tangible. The software was utilised as a digital forensics lab to composite, format, and prep all the physical assets for print, specifically the Character Profiles, Evidence Cards, and Location Tiles. Colour grading, noise overlays, and contrast adjustments were applied to all assets to simulate a cohesive, slightly degraded institutional aesthetic.

The most intensive phase involved constructing the visual hierarchy for the suspect character profiles. The layout was heavily regimented through a complex grid system to ensure it felt like an official institutional dossier. By pairing the subject's portrait with starkly categorised consumer data, such as Brand Associations and Lifestyle Indicators, the design intentionally strips the humanity from the suspects. This frames them as data points for the players to arbitrarily judge, reinforcing the project's core exploration of subconscious bias.
