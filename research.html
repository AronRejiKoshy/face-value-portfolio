DOSSIER A: ACADEMIC & THEORY

Motive: The Meaning System

The investigation begins with Naomi Klein’s No Logo (2000), establishing the primary motive: branding no longer operates as a simple commercial transaction. It has evolved into a cultural infrastructure. Corporations manufacture meaning, identity, and emotional association, shifting value away from material function and toward symbolic representation.

“Brands are not products, they are meaning systems.”

This intelligence directly informs the architecture of Face Value. The project strips away explicit brand identifiers, forcing players to navigate a reconstructed logic of controlled aesthetic cues. Suspects are not evaluated on hard evidence, but on lifestyle signals, tonal coherence, and visual packaging. The outcome proves that branding operates beneath conscious awareness, dictating trust and suspicion long before rational thought intervenes.

DOSSIER B: SOCIOLOGY & SEMIOTICS

Modus Operandi: Sign Value

To understand the mechanics of the deception, we turn to Jean Baudrillard’s The Consumer Society (1998). Baudrillard outlines the modus operandi: objects are valued not for their utility, but for what they signify. Within this framework, goods operate as a coded language, where worth is derived from status and symbolic association rather than factual substance.

“Consumption is a system of signification.”

This premise forms the core mechanic of Face Value. The suspects in the case file are not factual profiles; they are constructed sign systems. Players encounter curated cues, a profession, a specific visual tone, a carefully selected accessory, that imply a broader identity. These elements act as brand markers, signalling character without requiring explicit proof. The investigation reveals that players will readily convict or exonerate based entirely on symbolic logic rather than verifiable truth.

DOSSIER C: CONSUMER PSYCHOLOGY

The Attachment: Brand Psychology

While structural accounts emphasise symbolic systems, Susan Fournier’s Consumers and Their Brands (1998) reframes the investigation at the psychological level. Fournier identifies a critical mechanism in the deception: brands function not as inert objects, but as active, ‘viable relationship partners’ deeply embedded within a subject's identity narrative.

“Brand relationships contribute to identity formation and self-narrative construction.”

This psychological framing is directly exploited in Face Value. If branding contributes to identity construction and emotional investment at this relational level, then the symbolic hierarchies it produces do not merely inform consumption decisions, they dictate everyday social judgment. The game leverages these deep-seated brand relationships, proving that commercial signifiers are not just external signals of wealth, but psychological anchors used by the players to rapidly profile, categorise, and judge human subjects.

DOSSIER D: COGNITIVE PSYCHOLOGY

The Suspect: System 1

The primary suspect in flawed decision-making process is identified in Daniel Kahneman’s Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011). Kahneman profiles two modes of thought: System 1 and System 2. System 1 is the culprit—it operates automatically, relying on intuition, pattern recognition, and rapid association. It requires zero effort and rushes to immediate conclusions, unlike the slower, deliberate analysis of System 2.

“System 1 operates automatically and quickly, with little or no effort and no sense of voluntary control.”

Face Value is an environment engineered to weaponise System 1. Players are starved of concrete information and forced to make rapid deductions based solely on surface-level aesthetics. This mirrors the reality of modern social judgment, where deep analysis is rarely employed. The resulting evidence proves that bias is frequently unintentional, it is the inevitable byproduct of a brain forced to rely on automatic, associative shortcuts.

DOSSIER E: METHODOLOGY

The Procedure: Arts-Based Enquiry

To conduct this investigation, standard academic procedure was insufficient. Patricia Leavy’s Method Meets Art (2020) provides the procedural framework, arguing that creative practice, including interactive systems and games, is a rigorous mechanism for knowledge production. Instead of merely documenting findings, creative methods force active participation and emotional engagement.

“Art-based research can access forms of knowing that are otherwise unreachable.”

This directive is the foundation of Face Value. A written report could describe bias, but a playable system forces the participant to enact it. The board game operates as the primary research tool, gathering evidence through player interaction. Participants become active agents in the experiment, their choices mapping the exact trajectory of their unconscious assumptions. The project utilises game design not to illustrate theory, but to actively execute it in real-time.

DOSSIER F: EXHIBITION FIELD REPORT

The Precedent: Interactive Judgment

Field research conducted at the Serpentine Gallery identified a critical precedent in Danielle Brathwaite-Shirley’s The Delusion (2025). The exhibition deployed interactive multiplayer games designed to confront participants with their own ethical decision-making. Operating as a “human engine,” the gallery transformed visitors into active agents shaping live outcomes.

“What kind of views will you let in?”

A key finding emerged during the ‘Border’ game, where players dictated admission to a space based on severely limited intelligence, often just fragmented dialogue or appearance. Judgments were forced under pressure, mirroring the hostile environments of digital communication. The mechanic successfully exposed how exclusion is driven by instinct and assumption rather than verified facts.

This interaction validated the tactical approach for Face Value. By stripping players of objective truth and forcing reliance on curated, incomplete data, the system successfully extracts perception and bias. Face Value is not designed as a game of logical deduction, but as a system of exposure, proving that the choices made by the 'detectives' incriminate themselves more than the suspects they are investigating.

DOSSIER G: LUDOLOGY & MECHANICS

The Simulation: Weaponised Ambiguity

A crucial piece of operational intelligence was gathered through live simulation using the board game Deception: Murder in Hong Kong (2014). The system thrives on incomplete data and restricted communication. With hidden roles and heavily constrained visual clues, facts cannot be stated directly, meaning must be inferred, negotiated, and contested by the 'investigators.' It proves that within a group dynamic, hard evidence is secondary to persuasion, interpretation, and manufactured doubt.

“Truth is not revealed, it is constructed through suspicion.”

These mechanics directly informed the interrogation system in Face Value. Rather than providing an objective timeline, the project starves players of facts, forcing judgments based solely on fragments: visual cues, aesthetic descriptors, and implied narratives. Certainty is an illusion. Instead, the game exposes how group consensus and confident suggestion can entirely override logic. It mirrors the exact real-world conditions where bias successfully masquerades as rational judgment.

DOSSIER H: CINEMATIC INFLUENCE

The Misdirection: Narrative Profiling

The analysis of Rian Johnson’s Knives Out (2019) reveals a masterclass in misdirection. The film distributes suspicion unevenly, exploiting established character archetypes: wealthy elites, the outsider, the caregiver. Rather than relying on forensic clues, the narrative weaponises the audience's own implicit biases, proving how quickly guilt is assigned based purely on class, accent, and behaviour. Suspicion is manufactured through perception, not proof.

“I knew from the moment you got here you were guilty.”

This quote captures the exact mechanism being investigated: judgment precedes evidence. In Face Value, this tactic is fully deployed. Suspect profiles are built entirely on implication. Players receive no objective truth, only loaded signals, visual shorthand, socioeconomic cues, and curated context, designed to force an immediate conclusion. Like the film, the system exposes how 'logical deduction' is often just a rationalisation of our pre-existing prejudice.

DOSSIER I: CULTURAL DATA

The Pattern: Early Conditioning

The CPB London International Women’s Day campaign (2022) provides critical statistical intelligence on how deeply unconscious bias is embedded within everyday perception. By asking participants to interpret identical scenarios through simple drawings, the campaign exposed how assumptions regarding gender roles are solidified early and reinforced relentlessly. The results verify that bias is not a conscious moral decision, but a learned pattern of recognition that feels entirely natural to the subject.

“We all carry assumptions, the problem is we rarely question them.”

The data highlights the alarming speed at which these patterns emerge: 45% of children believed nurses are invariably female, while 22% exclusively associated doctors with men. Furthermore, 60% automatically identified trades such as plumbing as strictly male roles, and 46% believed men make inherently better engineers. These findings prove that prejudice is not developed later in life, it is actively programmed from childhood through repeated cultural signalling.

This intelligence fundamentally shaped Face Value. The project mimics these precise conditions, presenting players with limited, surface-level profiles and demanding immediate judgment. Like the campaign, the game demonstrates how perception is fully formed before conscious reasoning can intervene. By forcing players into this system, the experience proves that bias operates not as an anomaly, but as the default mode of human processing.

DOSSIER J: STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS

The Framework: Behavioural Engineering

Field surveillance at the Young V&A museum provided critical structural intelligence on analogue game systems. Analysing a spectrum of formats, from traditional grids to complex narrative simulations, the investigation shifted focus from the rules to the physical evidence: tiles, tokens, and spatial frameworks. The findings proved that meaning is not merely designed; it is constructed through physical interaction.

“Sometimes, games give players the chance to get creative — building worlds, telling stories, or making a game within a game.”

What emerged from this reconnaissance was a vital tactical understanding: board games are not fixed entertainment, but highly flexible behavioural frameworks. They are engineered environments capable of shaping suspect behaviour, guiding rapid decisions, and manufacturing meaning through forced participation.

This structural logic became the architectural foundation for Face Value. The project deploys this principle by creating an environment where the physical format itself forces interpretation and assumption. The investigation does not dictate the outcome; instead, the physical interaction acts as a tripwire, allowing the 'detectives' own behavioural patterns to expose their underlying bias.

DOSSIER K: PROCEDURAL LUDOLOGY

The Execution: Bureaucratic Bias

An operational analysis was conducted on Lucas Pope’s video game Papers, Please (2013). The system places the player in the role of an immigration officer. Through document inspection, extreme time pressure, and increasingly Byzantine rules, the game constructs an environment where decisions must be made rapidly on incomplete or conflicting intelligence. While the role appears strictly procedural, analysis reveals how suspects are reduced to data points, forcing players to abandon empathy in favour of rapid pattern recognition.

“Glory to Arstotzka.”

This mantra reinforces the authority of the system, demanding compliance over critical evaluation. This directly correlates to the mechanics of Face Value. Players are placed in a position of perceived authority, required to judge individuals based solely on superficial markers. Like the border checkpoint, the system appears highly objective, yet the act of judgment exposes how easily perception is compromised by pressure. What feels like a logical assessment is revealed to be a mere reflection of the system's design, proving that bias flourishes the moment human evaluation is reduced to an automated process.

DOSSIER L: METHODOLOGY

The Methodology: Psychological Profiling

The final investigation analysed the Netflix series Mindhunter (2017) to understand the early development of criminal profiling within the FBI. The series documents how institutional authorities categorise and predict offenders based on behavioural patterns. Through interviews with violent criminals, it demonstrates how identity is actively constructed through interpretation rather than established by absolute certainty. Profiling does not rely on complete evidence; it relies on fragments, speech, behaviour, background, which are assembled into cohesive psychological narratives.

“We’re not trying to understand the crime. We’re trying to understand the criminal.”

This operational shift from analysing actions to interpreting individuals directly informs Face Value. The project centres entirely on how subjects are read, categorised, and judged based on severely limited intelligence. Players are forced into a position where they must construct meaning from isolated fragments, relying on instinct and perceived patterns rather than verifiable proof. In doing so, the system exposes how profiling is never neutral. It is invariably shaped by bias, expectation, and cultural framing, ultimately proving that judgment is not a fact we discover, but a narrative we create.
