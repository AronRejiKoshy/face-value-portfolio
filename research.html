DOSSIER A: ACADEMIC & THEORY

Naomi Klein: No Logo

Naomi Klein’s No Logo (2000) reframes branding as a cultural system rather than a commercial tool. She argues that corporations no longer sell products, but instead produce meaning, identity, and emotional association. In this model, value shifts away from material function and toward symbolic representation. Branding becomes infrastructure: a framework through which individuals interpret authenticity, aspiration, and trust.

“Brands are not products, they are meaning systems.”

This idea directly informs Face Value. The project removes explicit brand identifiers, yet reconstructs branding logic through controlled aesthetic cues. Characters are not judged on evidence, but on symbolic signals, lifestyle, tone, and visual coherence. In doing so, the work demonstrates how branding operates beneath conscious awareness, shaping perception before rational thought occurs.

DOSSIER B: SOCIOLOGY & SEMIOTICS

Jean Baudrillard: Consumer Society

Jean Baudrillard’s The Consumer Society (1998) shifts the understanding of consumption away from utility and toward meaning. He argues that objects are not valued for what they do, but for what they signify. In this framework, goods operate within a system of signs, where value is produced through cultural codes, status, and symbolic association rather than function.

“Consumption is a system of signification.”

This concept is central to Face Value. The characters within the game are constructed as sign systems rather than factual profiles. Players are not given objective evidence, but instead encounter curated cues, profession, tone, visual style, that imply identity. These elements function like brands: they signal meaning without explicitly stating it. As a result, judgment is formed through interpretation rather than verification, revealing how perception is shaped by symbolic logic rather than truth.

DOSSIER C: MEDIA & CULTURE

Cinematic & Cultural Influences

To capture the authentic tension of a crime investigation, various films and series were analyzed. The pacing of these narratives directly informed how the "player" uncovers clues within the board game.

DOSSIER D: LUDOLOGY & MECHANICS

Game Design Research

Transitioning from a pure branding installation to a playable game required an investigation into ludology (the study of games). Research focused on rule-setting, player agency, and how mechanics can force players to confront their own unconscious biases.
