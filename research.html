DOSSIER A: ACADEMIC & THEORY

Naomi Klein: No Logo

Naomi Klein’s No Logo (2000) reframes branding as a cultural system rather than a commercial tool. She argues that corporations no longer sell products, but instead produce meaning, identity, and emotional association. In this model, value shifts away from material function and toward symbolic representation. Branding becomes infrastructure: a framework through which individuals interpret authenticity, aspiration, and trust.

“Brands are not products, they are meaning systems.”

This idea directly informs Face Value. The project removes explicit brand identifiers, yet reconstructs branding logic through controlled aesthetic cues. Characters are not judged on evidence, but on symbolic signals, lifestyle, tone, and visual coherence. In doing so, the work demonstrates how branding operates beneath conscious awareness, shaping perception before rational thought occurs.

DOSSIER B: SOCIOLOGY & SEMIOTICS

Jean Baudrillard: Consumer Society

Jean Baudrillard’s The Consumer Society (1998) shifts the understanding of consumption away from utility and toward meaning. He argues that objects are not valued for what they do, but for what they signify. In this framework, goods operate within a system of signs, where value is produced through cultural codes, status, and symbolic association rather than function.

“Consumption is a system of signification.”

This concept is central to Face Value. The characters within the game are constructed as sign systems rather than factual profiles. Players are not given objective evidence, but instead encounter curated cues, profession, tone, visual style, that imply identity. These elements function like brands: they signal meaning without explicitly stating it. As a result, judgment is formed through interpretation rather than verification, revealing how perception is shaped by symbolic logic rather than truth.

DOSSIER C: SEMIOTICS & MYTH

Roland Barthes: Mythologies

Roland Barthes’ Mythologies (1972) explores how everyday objects and images carry hidden cultural meanings. He introduces the concept of myth as a second-order sign system, where simple signs, such as names, visuals, or styles, become loaded with ideological meaning. Over time, these meanings appear natural, disguising the fact that they are socially constructed rather than inherent.

“Myth transforms history into nature.”

This framework is critical to Face Value. The project operates through surfaces that feel immediately understandable, a name, a profession, a visual cue, yet these elements are not neutral. They are constructed signs that trigger pre-learned associations. Players interpret these cues as if they were self-evident truths, rather than cultural meanings they have absorbed over time. In doing so, the game reveals how judgment is not based on evidence, but on myths that have become invisible through repetition.

DOSSIER D: COGNITIVE PSYCHOLOGY

Daniel Kahneman: Thinking, Fast and Slow

Daniel Kahneman’s Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011) distinguishes between two modes of thought: System 1 and System 2. System 1 operates automatically and rapidly, relying on intuition, pattern recognition, and association. It requires little effort and produces immediate judgments. In contrast, System 2 is slower, deliberate, and analytical, activated only when conscious reasoning is required.

“System 1 operates automatically and quickly, with little or no effort and no sense of voluntary control.”

This distinction is fundamental to Face Value. The game is deliberately structured to trigger System 1 thinking. Players are presented with limited information and required to make quick judgments based on surface-level cues. These conditions mirror real-world decision-making, where individuals rarely engage in extended analysis. As a result, choices are driven by instinctive associations rather than critical evaluation. The project exposes how easily perception is shaped under these conditions, revealing that bias is not always intentional, but often a byproduct of automatic thinking.
